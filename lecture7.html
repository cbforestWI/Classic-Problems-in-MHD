Part II
Classical Examples and Applications

Lecture 7
Hartmann Flow

Overview

Hartmann flow is the classic liquid-metal problem that teaches three MHD lessons at once.

1.
a magnetic field can strongly reshape a velocity profile even when magnetic advection is weak;
2.
the balance among pressure, viscosity, and Lorentz force naturally produces the Hartmann number;
3.
current closure at the walls is every bit as important as the bulk momentum balance.

Hartmann flow is one of the cleanest examples in all of MHD because the formal equations and the laboratory experiment sit very close together. In a liquid metal the assumptions of resistive–viscous MHD are often excellent, yet the magnetic Reynolds number can remain small. That makes this problem an ideal bridge between the general equations of the earlier lectures and a canonical laboratory flow.

Historical Perspective

The original analysis of pressure-driven flow in a transverse magnetic field goes back to Hartmann’s mercury experiments in the 1930s [Hartmann, 1937, Hartmann and Lazarus, 1937]. Hide and Roberts later turned these examples into textbook MHD problems with a clear physical interpretation [Hide and Roberts, 1962, Roberts, 1967]. Shercliff and Hunt showed how the story extends to real ducts, where sidewall current closure creates an additional family of boundary layers [Shercliff, 1956, Hunt, 1965].

Caution

Large magnetic influence does not mean ideal MHD. Many liquid-metal duct flows have \(\mathrm {Ha}\gg 1\) and yet \(\mathrm {Rm}\ll 1\). The magnetic field strongly brakes and reorganizes the flow, but the flux-freezing picture developed earlier is not the right language. Here diffusion of the induced field is fast, and wall-controlled current closure is central.

7.1 Geometry and low-\(\mathrm {Rm}\) ordering


Figure 7.1: Canonical Hartmann duct geometry. A pressure gradient drives flow along the duct while a transverse magnetic field brakes the motion through the induced current system. Figure adapted from Roberts [Roberts, 1967].

Take two parallel plates at \(x=\pm a\), infinite in \(y\) and extended along the duct direction \(z\). We impose a uniform magnetic field

\[\B _0 = B_0\,\hat{x} , \tag{7.1}\]
and drive a steady flow in the \(z\) direction with pressure gradient
\[G \equiv -\dd {p}{z} > 0. \tag{7.2}\]
The fully developed velocity is taken to be
\[\uvec = u_z(x)\,\hat{z} . \tag{7.3}\]

The usual assumptions are

  • steady state, \(\pp {}{t}=0\),
  • incompressibility, \(\divergence \uvec = 0\),
  • no-slip at the Hartmann walls, \(u_z(\pm a)=0\),
  • negligible variation in \(y\) and \(z\), so all gradients are in \(x\) only,
  • low magnetic Reynolds number, \(\mathrm {Rm}=Ua/\eta \ll 1\), using the definition from (4.3).

That last condition is the key difference from the flux-freezing lecture. Because \(\mathrm {Rm}\ll 1\), the magnetic field is mostly imposed rather than advected. We therefore write

\[\B = B_0\,\hat{x} + b_z(x)\,\hat{z} , \qquad \J = J_y(x)\,\hat{y} , \tag{7.4}\]
where \(b_z\) is a small induced correction. The governing equations are just the steady specialization of the momentum equation (1.8), the resistive Ohm law (1.9), and Ampère’s law (1.11).

7.2 Hydrodynamic limit: Poiseuille flow

If \(B_0=0\), the \(z\)-component of the steady momentum equation reduces to

\[\mu \dd {^2 u_z}{x^2} + G = 0. \tag{7.5}\]
Integrating twice, imposing symmetry \(\dd {u_z}{x}(0)=0\), and using the no-slip condition \(u_z(\pm a)=0\), one obtains the classical plane-Poiseuille profile
\[u_z(x) = \frac {G}{2\mu } \left (a^2-x^2\right ). \tag{7.6}\]
This is the profile to compare against once the Lorentz force is turned on.

7.3 Hartmann flow from the steady MHD equations

With the ansatz (7.3)–(7.4), the Lorentz force points along \(\hat{z} \) because

\[\J \times \B = J_y\,\hat{y} \times B_0\,\hat{x} = - J_y B_0\,\hat{z} . \tag{7.7}\]
and Ampère’s law gives
\[J_y = - \frac {1}{\muo } \dd {b_z}{x}. \tag{7.8}\]

We can search for steady solutions to the resistive, viscous MHD equations for this geometry with a transverse magnetic field \(b_x\). Note from \(\nabla \cdot \bm B= \frac {\partial b_x}{\partial x} = 0\) so that \(B_x=B_0\) a constant. the steady momentum equation becomes

\[-G + \frac {B_0}{\mu _0\rho } \frac {d b_x}{dz} + \mu \frac {d^2 u_x}{dz^2} = 0.\]

The \(z\)-momentum equation becomes

\[\mu \dd {^2 u_z}{x^2} - B_0 J_y + G = 0. \tag{7.10}\]
Meanwhile Ohm’s law gives
\[J_y = \sigma \left ( E_y + u_z B_0 \right ), \tag{7.11}\]

Substituting (7.11) into (7.10) yields a single second-order equation for the velocity,

\[\dd {^2 u_z}{x^2} - \frac {\sigma B_0^2}{\mu }u_z = \frac {\sigma E_y B_0 - G}{\mu }. \tag{7.12}\]
This form is more useful than the fourth-order elimination because it keeps the physics transparent: pressure drives the flow, viscosity smooths it, and the induced current brakes it.

Hartmann number. Using \(\mu =\rho \nu \) and \(\eta =(\muo \sigma )^{-1}\), define

\[\mathrm {Ha} \equiv B_0 a\sqrt {\frac {\sigma }{\rho \nu }} = \frac {V_A a}{\sqrt {\nu \eta }}, \qquad V_A \equiv \frac {B_0}{\sqrt {\muo \rho }}. \tag{7.13}\]
Then (7.12) reads
\[\dd {^2 u_z}{x^2} - \frac {\mathrm {Ha}^2}{a^2}u_z = \frac {\sigma E_y B_0 - G}{\mu }. \tag{7.14}\]

Solution. The general even solution of (7.14) is

\[u_z(x) = A\cosh \left (\frac {\mathrm {Ha}\,x}{a}\right ) + C. \tag{7.15}\]
A constant particular solution satisfies
\[- \frac {\mathrm {Ha}^2}{a^2}C = \frac {\sigma E_y B_0 - G}{\mu }, \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad C = \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{\sigma B_0^2}. \tag{7.16}\]
Applying \(u_z(\pm a)=0\) gives
\[A = - \frac {C}{\cosh (\mathrm {Ha})}, \tag{7.17}\]
so the Hartmann profile is
\[\boxed { u_z(x) = \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{\sigma B_0^2} \left [ 1- \frac {\cosh (\mathrm {Ha}\,x/a)}{\cosh (\mathrm {Ha})} \right ]. } \tag{7.18}\]
This is the basic textbook solution.

The current density follows immediately from Ohm’s law,

\[J_y(x) = \sigma E_y + \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{B_0} \left [ 1- \frac {\cosh (\mathrm {Ha}\,x/a)}{\cosh (\mathrm {Ha})} \right ]. \tag{7.19}\]
Integrating Ampère’s law then gives the induced magnetic field,
\[b_z(x) = -\muo \int _0^x J_y(x')\,dx', \tag{7.20}\]
with the additive constant fixed by symmetry or by a chosen reference level.

The cross-sectional average of the velocity is

\[\overline {u} \equiv \frac {1}{2a} \int _{-a}^{a} u_z(x)\,dx = \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{\sigma B_0^2} \left ( 1- \frac {\tanh (\mathrm {Ha})}{\mathrm {Ha}} \right ). \tag{7.21}\]
Equation (7.21) is the most useful form for engineering estimates because it connects the pressure gradient directly to the bulk flow.


Figure 7.2: Velocity and current profiles for classic Hartmann-flow electrical boundary conditions. These figures are especially useful for remembering that the wall physics selects \(E_y\), and \(E_y\) in turn selects the current pattern and the amount of magnetic braking [Roberts, 1967].

7.4 Limiting cases and physical interpretation

Weak magnetic field: \(\mathrm {Ha}\ll 1\). Expand the hyperbolic functions in (7.18):

\[1- \frac {\cosh (\mathrm {Ha}\,x/a)}{\cosh (\mathrm {Ha})} = \frac {\mathrm {Ha}^2}{2} \left (1-\frac {x^2}{a^2}\right ) + \mathcal {O}(\mathrm {Ha}^4). \tag{7.22}\]
Using (7.13), one recovers
\[u_z(x) \simeq \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{2\mu } \left (a^2-x^2\right ). \tag{7.23}\]
In the simplest conducting-wall textbook limit \(E_y=0\), this reduces to the Poiseuille result (7.6).

Strong magnetic field: \(\mathrm {Ha}\gg 1\). In the core, away from the walls,

\[u_z(x) \approx \frac {G-\sigma E_y B_0}{\sigma B_0^2}, \tag{7.24}\]
so the flow becomes plug-like. The shear is expelled into thin boundary layers of thickness
\[\delta _H \sim \frac {a}{\mathrm {Ha}}. \tag{7.25}\]
These are the Hartmann layers. In the common \(E_y=0\) limit, (7.21) becomes
\[\overline {u} = \frac {G}{\sigma B_0^2} \left ( 1- \frac {\tanh (\mathrm {Ha})}{\mathrm {Ha}} \right ), \tag{7.26}\]
and for \(\mathrm {Ha}\gg 1\) this implies the practical scaling
\[\frac {\Delta p}{L} = G \approx \sigma B_0^2\,\overline {u}. \tag{7.27}\]
This is why liquid-metal blanket concepts can suffer enormous MHD pressure drops even when the magnetic field is not frozen into the fluid.

What sets \(E_y\)? Equation (7.18) leaves \(E_y\) undetermined because it is fixed by current closure. In the simplest conducting-wall or externally short-circuited limit one often sets \(E_y=0\). For insulating sidewalls, by contrast, charge buildup creates an electrostatic field that partly cancels \(\uvec \times \B \) and reduces the current. The figures in Fig. 7.2 are useful precisely because they show how much this electrical boundary condition matters.

7.5 Dimensionless numbers and why liquid metals are special

Besides the Hartmann number, the standard control parameters are

\[\begin{aligned}\mathrm {Re} &\equiv \frac {Ua}{\nu }, & \mathrm {Rm} &\equiv \frac {Ua}{\eta }, & \mathrm {Pm} &\equiv \frac {\nu }{\eta } = \frac {\mathrm {Rm}}{\mathrm {Re}}, \\ N &\equiv \frac {\sigma B_0^2 a}{\rho U} = \frac {\mathrm {Ha}^2}{\mathrm {Re}}, & S &\equiv \frac {V_A a}{\eta }.\end{aligned} \tag{7.28}\]

Liquid metals typically have tiny magnetic Prandtl number \(\mathrm {Pm}\ll 1\). That is what makes the regime so instructive: one can have \(\mathrm {Rm}\ll 1\) while still reaching very large \(\mathrm {Ha}\) and \(N\). The field is not frozen in, but the Lorentz force still dominates the momentum balance.

Note

Experimental perspective. This is the regime that matters for liquid-metal MHD experiments and for fusion-blanket technology. In PbLi, sodium, or galinstan loops, one often worries less about flux freezing than about current closure, wall conductivity, and the pressure drop required to push the fluid through strong magnetic fields. The experimental moral is very practical: if the walls let currents close easily, the magnetic braking is severe; if the walls interrupt current closure, the pressure drop can be dramatically reduced.

7.6 From parallel plates to real ducts: Hartmann and Shercliff layers

The parallel-plate solution is only the first step. In a rectangular duct the core flow remains nearly uniform for large \(\mathrm {Ha}\), but two different boundary layers appear.

Hartmann layers. On walls whose normal is parallel to the imposed magnetic field, the local balance remains the Hartmann balance and the thickness is still

\[\delta _H \sim \frac {a}{\mathrm {Ha}}. \tag{7.30}\]

Shercliff layers. On sidewalls parallel to the imposed field, currents must turn and close through broader lateral layers. Their thickness scales as

\[\delta _S \sim \frac {a}{\sqrt {\mathrm {Ha}}}, \tag{7.31}\]
when the Hartmann number is defined with the Hartmann half-gap \(a\). These Shercliff layers are thicker than Hartmann layers by a factor of order \(\sqrt {\mathrm {Ha}}\).

Current closure through an electrostatic potential. For arbitrary duct cross sections it is often cleaner to solve for an electric potential \(\Phi \) rather than directly for the current. Writing

\[\E = -\grad \Phi , \qquad \J = \sigma \left (-\grad \Phi + \uvec \times \B \right ), \tag{7.32}\]
and using \(\divergence \J =0\) gives
\[\lap \Phi = \divergence (\uvec \times \B ). \tag{7.33}\]
The boundary conditions are what encode the wall physics: for a good conductor one often takes \(\Phi =\mathrm {const}\) on the wall, while for an insulator one imposes \(\J \cdot \hat {\vect {n}}=0\), or equivalently
\[\pp {\Phi }{n} = (\uvec \times \B )\cdot \hat {\vect {n}}. \tag{7.34}\]
Cross-section model solved by the explorer. This is a different coordinate choice from the earlier parallel-plate derivation above: here the applied field points along \(\hat {y}\), the flow is along \(\hat {z}\), and the only induced magnetic correction retained is the axial field \(b_z(x,y)\). The browser companion does not solve a separate transverse or poloidal magnetic-streamfunction problem. It works in the low-\(\mathrm {Rm}\) quasi-static limit with imposed field \(\B _0 = B_0\,\hat {y}\), and solves only for the streamwise velocity \(u_z(x,y)\) and electrostatic potential \(\Phi (x,y)\). Taking \(\uvec = u_z\hat {z}\) gives \(\uvec \times \B _0 = -u_z B_0\hat {x}\), so Ohm's law becomes
\[J_x = -\sigma \left (\pp {\Phi }{x} + u_z B_0\right ), \qquad J_y = -\sigma \pp {\Phi }{y}. \tag{7.35}\]
Enforcing \(\divergence \J = 0\) gives
\[\pp {^2 \Phi }{x^2} + \pp {^2 \Phi }{y^2} = -B_0\pp {u_z}{x}. \tag{7.36}\]
while the streamwise momentum balance reads
\[\mu \left (\pp {^2 u_z}{x^2} + \pp {^2 u_z}{y^2}\right ) - B_0 J_x + G = 0. \tag{7.37}\]
The explorer first solves this coupled \((u_z,\Phi )\) system. It then reconstructs the induced axial magnetic correction from Ampere's law, using
\[J_x = \frac {1}{\muo }\pp {b_z}{y}, \qquad J_y = -\frac {1}{\muo }\pp {b_z}{x}, \tag{7.38}\]
or equivalently
\[\lap _\perp b_z = \muo \left (\pp {J_x}{y} - \pp {J_y}{x}\right ), \tag{7.39}\]
with only an additive constant left free. No separate transverse or poloidal magnetic field is evolved in this reduced model. This is the real reason “duct flow” becomes more interesting than the parallel-plate problem: the three-dimensional current closure can dominate the qualitative behavior.

Takeaways
  • Hartmann flow is the canonical example of resistive–viscous MHD in the low-\(\mathrm {Rm}\) limit.
  • The Hartmann number measures how strongly the magnetic field flattens the profile and confines shear to boundary layers.
  • The average flow is controlled not just by pressure and viscosity, but by the wall-selected electric field \(E_y\) and therefore by the current-closure path.
  • In real ducts, Hartmann layers and Shercliff layers coexist; the former are thin, the latter broader, and both are central to liquid-metal MHD design.

Problems

Problem 7.1. Hartmann flow: PbLi in SPARC

Liquid metal breeding blankets often consider Pb–17Li (“PbLi”) flowing through rectangular ducts in a strong tokamak magnetic field. In this problem you will use Hartmann-flow scalings to estimate the MHD pressure drop, pumping power, and the boundary-layer thicknesses that must be resolved in simulations.

Given geometry and operating point Consider a straight rectangular duct of height \(h=10~\mathrm {cm}\) and width \(w=1~\mathrm {m}\) carrying PbLi at bulk speed \(U=1~\mathrm {m/s}\). Assume the magnetic field is uniform and transverse to the flow with magnitude \(B_0=10~\mathrm {T}\). Take the flow direction as \(\hat{z} \), and the field as \(\B _0 = B_0 \hat{x} \), so that the Hartmann walls are the two faces normal to \(\hat{x} \).

Treat the Hartmann gap as parallel plates separated by \(h\), so the half-gap is \[ a \equiv \frac {h}{2} = 0.05~\mathrm {m}. \]

Assume the textbook conducting-wall limit \(E_y=0\) in Ohm’s law, so that the mean-flow relation is given by (7.26).

Material properties (Pb–17Li, representative fusion temperature) Use the following representative values: \[ \rho \simeq 9.3\times 10^{3}\ \mathrm {kg/m^3}, \qquad \mu \simeq 1.4\times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm {Pa\cdot s}, \qquad \sigma \simeq 1.0\times 10^{6}\ \mathrm {S/m}. \] Compute the kinematic viscosity \(\nu =\mu /\rho \) and resistive diffusivity \(\eta = (\muo \sigma )^{-1}\).

Part A: dimensionless parameters and boundary layers

(a)
Compute the Reynolds number \(\mathrm {Re} = Ua/\nu \).
(b)
Compute the Hartmann number using (7.13).
(c)
For \(\mathrm {Ha}\gg 1\), estimate the Hartmann-layer thickness \(\delta _H \sim a/\mathrm {Ha}\).
(d)
(Optional.) Estimate the Shercliff-layer thickness δ_S ~ a/√Ha and compare it with δ_H.

Part B: pressure drop For the parallel-plate Hartmann solution with \(E_y=0\), the mean speed is related to the pressure gradient by \[ \overline {u} = \frac {G}{\sigma B_0^2} \left ( 1- \frac {\tanh (\mathrm {Ha})}{\mathrm {Ha}} \right ). \]

(a)
Solve for the required pressure gradient \(G= -dp/dz\) to achieve \(\overline {u}=U=1~\mathrm {m/s}\).
(b)
Evaluate the high-\(\mathrm {Ha}\) approximation \(\Delta p/L \approx \sigma B_0^2 U\) from (7.27) and compare it with the exact answer.
(c)
Report your final \(\Delta p/L\) in \(\mathrm {Pa/m}\) and in \(\mathrm {bar/m}\).

Part C: pumping power The volumetric flow rate is \(Q = U A\), where \(A = hw\) is the duct cross-sectional area.

(a)
Compute \(Q\).
(b)
The pumping power required over a length \(L\) is \(P_{\rm pump} = (\Delta p)Q\). Compute \(P_{\rm pump}\) for \(L=1~\mathrm {m}\).
(c)
Repeat for \(L=10~\mathrm {m}\) and comment on what this implies about the value of electrical insulation in liquid-metal blanket concepts.

Short conceptual questions

(a)
Why does the \(E_y=0\) assumption permit a large Lorentz force and therefore a large pressure drop?
(b)
Why do large-\(\mathrm {Ha}\) flows look plug-like in the core even though the strongest gradients are confined to thin boundary layers?

Sanity-check hint (order of magnitude). A consistent estimate gives \(\mathrm {Ha}\gg 1\), a Hartmann-layer thickness in the few-\(\mu \mathrm {m}\) range, and an MHD pressure drop of order \(10^8~\mathrm {Pa/m}\) for \(E_y=0\), implying pumping power of order \(\sim 10~\mathrm {MW}\) per meter of duct at the stated flow rate.
